Article Used: American Bully XL ban would punish well behaved pets, Dogs Trust warns

Response:

 This article covers dialogue around the potential banning of a dog breed. The breed, dubbed the “XL Bully” is a pit bull-like dog breed that has been responsible for “75% of all fatal dog attacks in the UK in the past three years.” The recent controversy came after one of the XL Bully dogs attacked an 11-year- old girl in Birmingham this past Saturday. Now, the girl and many other government and community officials are hoping to see heavy restrictions if not an outright ban on the breed. Politician Kit Malthouse, who has a history of controlling the ownership of dangerous dogs, plans to introduce legislation that would ban the sale, import, and breeding of the breed as well as introduce “neuter and muzzle” orders for current owners. However, others claim that this level of restriction is problematic. Owen Sharp, an executive at a UK dog charity, said that not only would a ban be impractical, but it would also punish responsible families by taking away their well-behaved pets. Others, such as XL Bully owner Sophie Coulthard, call for breeding restrictions instead.

 This article is interesting as it closely mirrors dialogue in the US over breeds like pit bulls or Rottweilers. In the US too we see cycles of someone being attacked by a pit bull and subsequent calls for restrictions over the dog. Interestingly, there doesn’t seem to be huge support in the US for sweeping restrictions like those seen in the Telegraph article. On the other hand, while unscientific, a poll of readers inside the article showed an 80-20 split in favor of banning the XL Bully breed outright.

Article Used: Rishi Sunak and Jeremy Hunt in talks to scrap phase two of HS2 as costs spiral

Response:

The article I chose to discuss for this assignment is an article from The Independent entitled “Rishi Sunak and Jeremy Hunt in Talks to Scrap Phase Two of HS2 as Costs Spiral”. As the title of the article suggests, this article concerns the rumor of talks that have been occurring between Rishi Sunak (Prime Minister of the U.K.) and Jeremy Hunt (Chancellor of the Exchequer ) regarding the ambitious but time consuming HS2 rail project.

 The HS2 rail project is a project to construct a rail line throughout a series of major cities in the U.K.. The idea of the project is to bring the biggest cities in the U.K. closer together through this new method of transportation that “will boost productivity, and will provide a low-carbon alternative to cars and planes for many decades to come” (Stone). In addition to this, the railway is predicted to dramatically cut down on north to south intercity travel times and to free up capacity on other operating service lines. This project was broken up into two distinct phases, neither of which have been completed yet. The first phase of the project was initially set to open in 2026. Now, thanks to delays, the entire project likely won’t be finished until 2029 (Stone).

 There is a political element to this situation too, and these politics all surround money. As one might imagine, such an endeavor would be quite expensive to achieve. According to The Independent who claims to have seen a cost estimate for the project, the U.K. government has already spent a total of £2.3bn on stage two of this project (which stretches from Birmingham to Manchester) and is expected to spend another £34bn in finishing that phase. In response to this wildly high cost estimate, some political parties (particularly the Tory MPs) have openly suggested to the Prime Minister that he put an end to the project upon the completion of phase one. The situation with the project is also concerning for the Chancellor as he faces pressure from the Tory MPs to implement tax cuts before the next election cycle. Amidst these concerns and others such as the U.K. government’s “infrastructure watchdog”’s warning in July that the project might be “unachievable”, it appears as though the Prime Minister and Chancellor have entered talks that may lead to the scrapping of the second part of phase two (called phase 2b) as phase 2a has already been approved by parliament and would be difficult to stop (Stone).

 I found this article very interesting and insightful as it gives a peek behind the curtain regarding some of the U.K.’s current government innerworkings. This article reveals that, similar to the U.S., the topic of government budgets is very prevalent in the U.K.. The idea of a massive government sponsored project that quickly gets out of control and begins eating away at tons of money is an idea that Americans know all too well, so it is nice to see that the U.K. also encounters these same issues.

 The other thing that I found fascinating about this article was the idea that citizens in parliament seem to have open access to ask the Prime Minister questions on a semi-regular basis. The article mentions that Michael Fabricant, representing the Tory MPs, asked the Prime Minister to scrap phase two of the HS2 point blank at an event called “Prime Minister’s Questions” (Stone). Doing a quick Google search reveals that Prime Minister’s Questions is a ticketed event that takes place every Wednesday at noon where members of parliament can ask the Prime Minister questions regarding policy while U.K. citizens (and also foreign visitors, if space permits) can watch (Prime Minister’s Questions). This event intrigues me as it demonstrates a willingness of the Prime Minister to grant others frequent open access to himself. This event also seems to serve as a way for regular citizens of the U.K. to watch and get an understanding of how the U.K. government operates which seems like an incredibly unique opportunity that a lot of other countries’ governments do not offer.
